# Mental Health in Practice: Neuroscience-Based Habits Inspired by Eslen Delanogare

You wake up tired. Before your feet touch the floor, your hand reaches for your phone. Messages, notifications, news, tasks, comparisons — all of it enters your mind before you have even taken a full breath. You start the day already overwhelmed, promise yourself you will change tomorrow, and by night, you feel mentally exhausted again.

This cycle is familiar to many people. It also reveals something important: mental health is not only about treating crises when anxiety, burnout, or depression become unbearable. It is also built through the small choices we repeat every day — how we sleep, eat, move, work, rest, use technology, regulate emotions, and relate to ourselves.

Eslen Delanogare, a Brazilian psychologist and neuroscientist, has become known for discussing mental health, discipline, behavior change, emotional regulation, and lifestyle through a practical neuroscience lens. Inspired by this approach, this article explores how small, consistent habits can support emotional balance, productivity, and quality of life.

This is not a replacement for therapy, diagnosis, or medical care. Instead, it is a practical guide to understanding how everyday behaviors influence mental well-being.

---

## Mental Health Is Built Daily, Not Only Treated in Crisis

Many people think seriously about mental health only when they are already suffering: anxious, emotionally drained, burned out, numb, or unable to function as usual. But mental health works a lot like physical health. We do not take care of the body only when illness appears; we maintain it through sleep, nutrition, movement, prevention, and awareness. The mind deserves the same kind of daily attention.

Being mentally healthy does not mean feeling happy, calm, or motivated all the time. Sadness, fear, anger, stress, and frustration are normal parts of life. Mental health is more about having the internal structure to recognize what you feel, regulate your reactions, ask for help when needed, and continue functioning in a healthier way. It is not emotional perfection — it is emotional capacity.

The brain responds strongly to repeated patterns. What you do every day shapes your mood, motivation, attention, and resilience. One good night of sleep helps, but consistent sleep is more powerful. Exercising once a month is better than nothing, but regular movement changes the body and brain more deeply. A single meditation session may bring relief, but consistent reflection can train emotional regulation over time.

Waking and sleeping at regular times, reducing excessive screen use, moving your body, eating in a way that supports energy, creating moments of silence, and staying connected to supportive people are simple habits that gradually strengthen mental health. Of course, mental well-being is influenced by biological, psychological, social, and environmental factors — not willpower alone. Still, daily habits can create a foundation that supports healing, stability, and resilience.

## The Brain Needs Structure: Discipline, Routine, and Emotional Stability

Your brain spends energy making decisions all day. What should I do first? Should I check my phone? Should I exercise? Should I study now or later? When life has no structure, every small action becomes a negotiation. Predictable routines reduce cognitive overload because the brain knows what to expect. This creates a sense of stability that can lower stress and make healthy choices easier.

Discipline is often misunderstood as punishment, pressure, or rigidity. But from a mental health perspective, discipline can be a form of self-care. It protects your long-term well-being from short-term impulses. Motivation is useful, but it is unreliable. It changes with sleep quality, stress, hormones, emotions, environment, and the people around you. If you wait to “feel like it” before doing what matters, you may stay stuck for a long time.

A mental health-friendly routine does not have to be extreme. In the morning, you might wake up, get sunlight, drink water, move your body, and avoid social media for the first few minutes of the day. During work or study, you can use focused blocks, planned breaks, and less multitasking. At night, you might reduce stimulation, prepare for the next day, disconnect from screens, and create a calming ritual.

The key is to start small: five minutes of exercise, one page of reading, two minutes of breathing, one short walk. Discipline should support your life, not become another source of stress. A routine that is too rigid can create pressure and frustration; the goal is consistency with flexibility, not perfection.

## Lifestyle Foundations: Sleep, Exercise, Nutrition, and Sunlight

Mental health is not separate from the body. The brain is part of the body, and it depends on biological conditions to function well. This is why lifestyle is one of the strongest foundations for emotional regulation. Sleep, movement, nutrition, and light exposure do not solve every mental health problem, but they create a better environment for the brain to manage stress, focus, and emotions.

### Sleep as Emotional Regulation

Poor sleep affects almost everything: mood, irritability, anxiety, focus, memory, and impulse control. When you are sleep-deprived, problems feel bigger, patience becomes smaller, and emotions are harder to regulate. It is not just “being tired” — it is your nervous system operating with fewer resources.

Practical sleep habits include keeping a consistent bedtime and wake-up time, avoiding caffeine late in the day, reducing screen exposure before bed, and keeping the room dark, cool, and quiet. A wind-down routine can also help signal to the brain that the day is ending. If sleep problems are chronic or severe, it is important to discuss them with a healthcare professional.

### Exercise as a Mental Health Tool

Physical activity supports brain health by improving blood flow, neurochemical balance, stress regulation, energy, and self-esteem. It can also provide a sense of progress and agency, which is especially valuable when life feels chaotic or emotionally heavy.

Exercise does not need to be extreme. Walking, cycling, dancing, swimming, strength training, stretching, or playing a sport can all help. Consistency matters more than intensity. Try 20 to 30 minutes of walking most days, strength training two or three times per week, or short movement breaks during long work periods.

### Nutrition and the Brain

Food influences energy, concentration, and mood. This does not mean nutrition is a cure for anxiety, depression, trauma, or other mental disorders. But what you eat can either support or weaken your mental functioning.

A balanced approach is best: eat enough protein, include fruits, vegetables, and fiber, stay hydrated, and avoid relying heavily on ultra-processed foods. Pay attention to how certain meals affect your energy, digestion, and mood. The goal is not perfection — it is giving your brain steady fuel.

### Sunlight and Biological Rhythm

Natural light helps regulate the circadian rhythm, the internal clock that influences alertness during the day and sleepiness at night. Morning sunlight, in particular, can help the body understand when it is time to wake up and when it is time to prepare for rest later.

Simple habits can make a difference: go outside in the morning when possible, open windows and curtains early, take short outdoor breaks, and reduce bright artificial light late at night. These small environmental signals help your body and mind stay more synchronized.

## Anxiety, Dopamine, and the Problem of Constant Stimulation

Modern life surrounds us with stimulation. Smartphones, social media, notifications, streaming platforms, short videos, and endless information train the brain to seek quick rewards. Scrolling, checking messages, eating for comfort, and avoiding difficult tasks can become automatic ways to escape discomfort.

Dopamine is involved in motivation, reward, and pursuit. In simple terms, when the brain gets used to frequent quick stimulation, slower and more meaningful tasks can begin to feel boring or difficult. Reading, studying, exercising, working deeply, or sitting quietly with your thoughts may seem unbearable compared to the instant reward of a screen. Over time, overstimulation can reduce attention span, increase restlessness, intensify comparison, worsen procrastination, and make anxiety feel louder.

Technology is not the enemy. It is a tool that needs boundaries. Try avoiding your phone during the first 30 minutes after waking, turning off non-essential notifications, creating screen-free periods, keeping your phone away during focused work, and using social media intentionally instead of automatically.

It is also helpful to allow some boredom. Boredom creates space for reflection, creativity, and emotional processing. Many people use constant stimulation to avoid sadness, loneliness, stress, or insecurity. Mental health improves when we learn to feel and process emotions instead of immediately escaping them.

When difficult feelings appear, practice naming the emotion, breathing deeply, writing your thoughts down, talking to someone trustworthy, taking a walk before reacting, or simply observing what you feel without rushing to distract yourself.

## Self-Knowledge, Therapy, and the Courage to Ask for Help

Habits are powerful, but they do not replace deeper emotional work. Mental health also requires self-knowledge: understanding your triggers, recognizing repeated patterns, identifying limiting beliefs, and noticing how past experiences influence current behavior. Sometimes the problem is not only that you sleep poorly or use your phone too much. Sometimes there are wounds, fears, relationship patterns, or unresolved conflicts underneath.

Therapy can help you understand your thoughts, emotions, relationships, and behaviors more clearly. It can provide tools for emotional regulation, decision-making, communication, and healing. It can also support treatment for anxiety, depression, trauma, grief, compulsions, and many other issues. Seeking professional help is not weakness. It is a responsible act of self-care.

You may benefit from professional support if you experience persistent sadness or hopelessness, constant anxiety or panic, loss of interest in activities, major sleep or appetite changes, difficulty functioning at work, school, or in relationships, excessive irritability, emotional numbness, or thoughts of self-harm.

If you are experiencing thoughts of suicide or self-harm, seek immediate help from emergency services, a crisis hotline, or a trusted person nearby. Personal responsibility matters, but compassion matters too. Mental health is complex, and improvement often requires patience, support, and multiple strategies working together.

## Conclusion: Build a Life That Supports Your Mind

Mental health is shaped by what we repeatedly do. Your brain needs sleep, movement, structure, meaningful relationships, emotional awareness, and healthy boundaries with stimulation. These habits do not make life perfect, but they give your nervous system better conditions to handle stress, uncertainty, and emotion.

Inspired by Eslen Delanogare’s practical neuroscience-based perspective, we can understand discipline, routine, and self-awareness not as rigid rules, but as tools for freedom. When your life has supportive structure, you rely less on unstable motivation and more on systems that protect your well-being.

Change does not need to begin dramatically. It can start with one better night of sleep, one walk, one therapy appointment, one honest conversation, or one hour away from your phone.

Choose one area of your mental health to strengthen this week: sleep, exercise, screen boundaries, routine, or emotional reflection. Start small, stay consistent, and seek professional support when needed. Your mind is not separate from your life — it is shaped by the way you live every day.
